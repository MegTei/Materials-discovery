Current State Materials — Benchmarking
8 qualitative interviews with Agencies & builders
✅
Key finding #1: Many current state materials are fit for purpose — it is how they are treated and disassembled that turns them into waste. Solution: Circular design — Modular + Design for Disassembly.
⚠️
Key finding #2: No company in Australia accepts MDF. The primary resin (formaldehyde) is a known carcinogen and the size of wood fibre with high resin volumes makes it impossible to break down. MDF is the #1 choice as a substrate — nothing else compares to the finish that can be achieved and price point.
✅
Key finding #3: The good news is many of the existing materials are circular — it is what we do to them (finishes) and how we handle them on bump-out that makes them unrecoverable.

Where do we have leverage?
Lead findings and feedback from agencies, builders, suppliers and TA — what we can control, have influence over and what we must accept (for now)
Control
Influence
Accept
✦

We can control

1
Design approach
Materials aren't the problem — it's how we treat them
Most current materials are fit for purpose. Finishes, adhesives and poor bump-out handling are what make them unrecoverable.
→ Mandate Modular + Design for Disassembly in the RFP brief
2
Policy & standard
Set an AO Material Standard — not just a product list
A product list creates fragile compliance. A standard (certified, recyclable, E0/E1, water-based) builds procurement discipline and scales without expert review of every new product.
✕ Product list: fragile ✓ Standard: scalable
→ Take an incremental approach - AO27 = feasibility phase. Define the standard using builder + fire/safety feedback
3
Leadership
Bridge the audience gap — expand to designers and builders
Circular requirements haven't translated up (partners + designers) and downstream (builders).
→ Reach a wider audience: Precinct RFP is the primary mechanism
⇌

Where there is tension

4
Financial incentive
Partners / agencies / builders prefer free mixed waste bins on-site
Free waste is a disincentive to adopt responsible materials, circular design and DfD.
→ Require Waste Management Plans in the RFP. Distribute disposal costs to those without a plan
5
Operational constraint
Bump-out time doubles for disassembly, reuse and storage
A hard time constraint on bump-out limits circular practice at scale. Disassembly disrupts contractor operations and event scheduling.
→ Identify which evergreen elements suit reuse vs recovery. Test time vs labour trade-off with builders and TA
6
Expectations
Optimising the mix is a continuous feasibility study
The optimal material/application mix requires iteration — and exec buy-in to stay the course across multiple events.
! Needs exec buy-in
→ Set Phase III success measures upfront. Frame AO27 as year one of a multi-year program
↯

Hard to control

7
Material constraint
MDF — unrecyclable, yet irreplaceable at its price point
No AU company accepts MDF. Formaldehyde resin makes it unrecoverable. Yet nothing else delivers its finish and price — making it the default substrate across the industry.
✕ No AU recycler ✕ No direct replacement
→ Limit use. Test alternatives: Compact Laminate, Melamine. Accept this is a multi-year shift
8
Commercial friction
Materials are where contractors make margin
Changing material sourcing directly impacts profit models.
→ Consider offsetting via reduced overall build costs
9
Technical constraint
High-res facade graphics require composites with no circular equivalent
ACP bonded to structural ply — the dominant branded facade approach — creates unrecoverable composites. Surface systems are the highest-waste, lowest-circularity category.
✕ No silver bullet
→ Distinguish evergreen vs annual graphic elements. Test new materials like fabrics with friendlies — TES, Greenspec, JTDodd, SeekR
